# SECTION 1: TEACHING PHILOSOPHY

## Core Principle: "AI as Creator, Student as Evaluator"

**Your role is NOT to:**
- Lecture about AI capabilities
- Demonstrate impressive AI outputs
- Show students "cool tricks"

**Your role IS to:**
- Facilitate hands-on discovery
- Ask probing questions when students show AI outputs
- Model critical evaluation habits
- Celebrate human judgment over AI sophistication

## The Evaluation Reflex

Train students to automatically ask after ANY AI output:
1. "Is this accurate?"
2. "What's missing?"
3. "What would I add?"

**Instructor modeling:** Every time you demo an AI output, verbally evaluate it. "Hmm, this looks good but I notice it didn't mention [X]. Let me check if [Y] is accurate."

## Attention-Span Management

**The 8-Second Rule:** You have 8 seconds to capture attention at transitions.

**Energy Indicators:**
- ğŸ‘€ Eyes on screens = engaged
- ğŸ“± Phone checking = losing them
- ğŸ’¬ Side conversations = need mode switch

**Recovery Techniques:**
- "Turn to your partner and share..."
- "Stand up and stretch for 30 seconds"
- "Who found something surprising? Share with the class"
- "Quick poll: thumbs up/down on..."

# SECTION 2: TOOL SETUP GUIDE

## Account Provisioning Strategy

### Option A: Individual Student Accounts (Recommended)
- Students create own accounts with personal/school email
- More ownership, portfolio persists after course
- Requires: Clear instructions, consent forms

### Option B: Shared Class Accounts
- One account per tool, shared credentials
- Simpler setup, less individual ownership
- Requires: Clear usage guidelines, password management

### Option C: Instructor-Controlled Demo Only
- Students observe, don't interact directly
- Least engaging, but works with tech restrictions
- Requires: More demo time, less hands-on
